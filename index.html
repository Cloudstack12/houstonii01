Medigap vs. Medicare Advantage: Which Plan Provides the Best Value?


Medicare, a federal health insurance program in the United States, provides coverage for individuals aged 65 and older, specifically younger individuals with disabilities. While Medicare offers comprehensive healthcare benefits, there are still gaps in coverage that may leave beneficiaries responsible for significant out-of-pocket expenses. This is where additional coverage options like Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans come into play.

Comparing the value of Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans is crucial for individuals seeking to enhance their Medicare coverage. Understanding each plan's differences, benefits, and costs can help beneficiaries make an informed decision that aligns with their unique healthcare needs and financial circumstances. In this article, we will delve into the comparison of Medigap vs. Medicare Advantage plans, exploring the value provided by each option and helping you determine which plan might be the best fit for you.

Understanding Medigap Plans

Medicare provides essential health coverage for millions of Americans but doesn't cover all healthcare expenses. This is where Medigap plans, also known as Medicare Supplement Insurance, come into play. Medigap plans are private insurance policies that work alongside Original Medicare to help fill the gaps in coverage and reduce out-of-pocket costs. Let's take a closer look at Medigap plans' purpose, scope, and benefits, how they work alongside Original Medicare, and their pros and cons.


Definition and Purpose of Medigap Plans

Medigap plans are standardized insurance policies offered by private insurance companies. These plans are designed to supplement Original Medicare (Part A and Part B) by covering certain costs that Medicare doesn't cover, such as deductibles, copayments, and coinsurance. The government regulates Medigap plans and must adhere to federal and state laws to ensure consistency in benefits and coverage.


The purpose of Medigap plans is to provide beneficiaries with greater financial protection and peace of mind. In addition, they help bridge the gaps left by Original Medicare, allowing individuals to have more predictable healthcare costs and access to a broader network of healthcare providers.


Coverage and Benefits Provided by Medigap Plans

Medigap plans offer a range of standardized coverage options labeled with letters A to N. While the specific benefits may vary depending on the plan, some common coverage areas include:

  • Part A coinsurance and hospital costs: Medigap plans may cover the coinsurance and additional hospital costs that Medicare Part A doesn't cover.

  • Part B coinsurance or copayments: Medigap plans can help pay for the 20% coinsurance or copayments for Medicare Part B services.

  • Blood: Medigap plans often cover the first three pints of blood needed for a medical procedure.

  • Skilled nursing facility care coinsurance: Some Medigap plans cover the coinsurance for professional nursing facility care, extending coverage beyond Medicare's limited period.

  • Part A hospice care coinsurance or copayments: Medigap plans can help cover the coinsurance or copayments for hospice care under Medicare Part A.

Please note that Medigap plans do not include prescription drug coverage. Therefore, beneficiaries must enroll in a separate Medicare Part D plan to obtain prescription drug coverage.

How Medigap Plans Work Alongside Original Medicare

Medigap plans work in conjunction with Original Medicare. Here's how they complement each other:

  • Medicare pays its share: Original Medicare (Part A and Part B) covers its portion of the approved healthcare costs.

  • Medigap pays its share: After Medicare pays its claim, the Medigap plan covers the remaining out-of-pocket costs according to the plan's benefits.

  • Freedom to choose providers: Medigap plans generally allow beneficiaries to visit any healthcare provider nationwide who accepts Medicare patients, giving individuals more flexibility in selecting doctors and specialists.

It's important to note that Medigap plans cannot be used with Medicare Advantage plans. Therefore, if you have a Medigap plan and decide to switch to a Medicare Advantage plan, you must discontinue your Medigap coverage.


Exploring Medicare Advantage Plans

Medicare Advantage plans, or Medicare Part C, are comprehensive healthcare options offered by private insurance companies approved by Medicare. These plans provide an alternative way to receive Medicare benefits, often incorporating additional coverage and benefits beyond what Original Medicare offers. In this article, we will delve into the definition and purpose of Medicare Advantage plans, the coverage and benefits they provide, the different types of Medicare Advantage plans available, how they differ from Original Medicare and Medigap, and their pros and cons.


The purpose of Medicare Advantage plans is to offer a simplified and integrated healthcare experience. These plans aim to provide beneficiaries with a broader range of benefits, including prescription drug coverage, dental care, vision care, and wellness programs, all bundled into one plan.


Coverage and Benefits Provided by Medicare Advantage Plans

Medicare Advantage plans offer a range of coverage and benefits beyond what Original Medicare provides. While the specific benefits may vary depending on the plan and the insurance provider, some common coverage areas include:

  • Hospital care (Part A): Medicare Advantage plans typically cover the hospital care included in Medicare Part A, such as inpatient hospital stays, skilled nursing facility care, and hospice care.

  • Medical services (Part B): Medicare Advantage plans cover the medical services included in Medicare Part B, such as doctor visits, preventive care, outpatient care, and durable medical equipment.

  • Prescription drug coverage (Part D): Many Medicare Advantage plans include prescription drug coverage, helping beneficiaries manage their medication costs.

  • Additional benefits: Medicare Advantage plans often provide other benefits not covered by Original Medicare, such as dental care, vision care, hearing aids, fitness programs, and wellness services.

Different Types of Medicare Advantage Plans

There are different types of Medicare Advantage plans, each with its own characteristics and provider networks. Some common types include:

  • Health Maintenance Organization (HMO) plans: HMO plans require beneficiaries to select a primary care physician and obtain referrals for specialist visits. They typically have a network of healthcare providers and require individuals to receive care within that network, except for emergencies.

  • Private Fee-for-Service (PFFS) plans: PFFS plans determine how much they will pay for covered services and how much beneficiaries are responsible for. These plans may have a network of providers, but they also allow individuals to seek care from any provider that accepts the plan's terms.

  • Special Needs Plans (SNPs): SNPs cater to individuals with specific health conditions or special needs, providing specialized coverage tailored to their requirements.

How Medicare Advantage Plans Differ from Original Medicare and Medigap

Medicare Advantage plans differ from Original Medicare and Medigap in several ways:

  • Coverage structure: Medicare Advantage plans to combine the benefits of Medicare Part A and Part B into one plan, often including additional coverage such as prescription drugs. Original Medicare, in contrast, consists of separate Part A and Part B coverage. Medigap plans work alongside Original Medicare to fill gaps in coverage.

  • Provider networks: Medicare Advantage plans typically have networks of healthcare providers, and beneficiaries may need to receive care within those networks. Original Medicare allows individuals to choose any healthcare provider that accepts Medicare. Therefore, Medigap plans generally do not have specific provider networks.

  • Cost-sharing: Medicare Advantage plans often have cost-sharing arrangements, such as copayments or service coinsurance. Original Medicare and Medigap plans have standardized cost-sharing structures that may differ from those of Medicare Advantage plans.

  • Additional benefits: Medicare Advantage plans frequently offer other benefits like prescription drug coverage, dental care, vision care, and wellness programs. Original Medicare and Medigap plans do not typically include these extras.

It's essential for individuals considering Medicare Advantage plans to carefully evaluate their specific healthcare needs, preferences, and financial circumstances to determine if these plans align with their requirements.


Medicare Advantage plans offer a comprehensive alternative to Original Medicare, with additional benefits and coverage options. By understanding how these plans differ from Original Medicare and Medigap, individuals can make informed decisions regarding their healthcare coverage and choose the plan that best suits their needs.

Cost Considerations

One of the critical factors in evaluating the value of any healthcare plan is the cost. When comparing Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans, consider the following cost considerations:

  • Premiums: Medigap plans generally have higher monthly premiums than Medicare Advantage plans. However, Medicare Advantage plans may sometimes have lower or even zero premiums.

  • Deductibles and Copayments: Medigap plans often cover deductibles and copayments, reducing or eliminating out-of-pocket costs. On the other hand, Medicare Advantage plans may have lower deductibles and copayments but may still require beneficiaries to pay a portion of the costs.

  • Out-of-Pocket Expenses: Medigap plans typically have higher predictability regarding out-of-pocket expenses. With Medicare Advantage plans, out-of-pocket costs can vary depending on the plan, such as copayments, coinsurance, and annual maximums.

Coverage and Flexibility

Another aspect to consider is the extent of coverage and flexibility provided by the plans:

  • Coverage: Medigap plans work alongside Original Medicare and cover services that Medicare doesn't cover, such as deductibles, copayments, and coinsurance. On the other hand, Medicare Advantage plans replace Original Medicare and offer comprehensive coverage that includes Medicare Parts A, B, and often Part D (prescription drugs).

  • Freedom to Choose Providers: Medigap plans generally allow beneficiaries to see any healthcare provider nationwide who accepts Medicare. However, Medicare Advantage plans often have provider networks, and beneficiaries may need to choose healthcare providers within those networks, except in emergencies.

Prescription Drug Coverage

Prescription drug coverage is a significant consideration when comparing the value of Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans:

  • Medigap plans do not typically include prescription drug coverage. Beneficiaries with Medigap plans must enroll in a separate Medicare Part D plan to get coverage for prescription drugs.

  • Medicare Advantage plans often include prescription drug coverage as part of their comprehensive benefits. The availability, formulary, and costs of prescription drugs can vary among Medicare Advantage plans, so it's essential to review the specifics of each plan.

Making an Informed Decision

Making an informed decision is crucial when choosing between Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans. Evaluate Your Individual Needs and Preferences

Every individual has unique healthcare needs and preferences. To make an informed decision, take the following steps:

  • Assess your current health status: Consider your medical conditions, ongoing treatments, and anticipated healthcare needs. Determine if you require specialized care, prescription medications, or frequent doctor visits.

  • Consider your healthcare provider preferences: Reflect on whether you have relationships with specific doctors, specialists, or healthcare facilities you want to maintain. Evaluate the importance of having the freedom to choose healthcare providers without network restrictions.

  • Reflect on your budget and financial capabilities: Determine your budget for healthcare expenses, including premiums, deductibles, copayments, and potential out-of-pocket costs. Assess your ability to handle unpredictable costs versus having more predictable costs.

Tips for Comparing and Selecting the Right Plan

To compare and select the plan that provides the best value for your needs, consider the following tips:

  • Research and gather information: Utilize reputable sources such as official Medicare websites, insurance company resources, and independent consumer guides to understand the features, benefits, and costs of Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans.

  • Compare costs: Evaluate the premiums, deductibles, copayments, and out-of-pocket expenses associated with each plan. Consider your healthcare usage and determine which plan offers the most cost-effective option based on your anticipated healthcare needs.

  • Assess coverage and benefits: Compare the coverage provided by each plan, including the extent of coverage for medical services, prescription drugs, and additional benefits such as dental and vision care. Determine which program aligns best with your specific healthcare requirements.

  • Review provider networks: If you value the freedom to choose healthcare providers, consider the provider networks associated with Medicare Advantage plans. Ensure that the plan's network includes your preferred doctors, specialists, and healthcare facilities.

  • Consider prescription drug coverage: If you require prescription medications, evaluate the availability and costs of prescription drug coverage in Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans. Determine which plan offers the most suitable range for your medication needs.

Making an informed decision about Medigap and Medicare Advantage plans involves evaluating your needs, preferences, and long-term considerations.
